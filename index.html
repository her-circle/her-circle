✦ Who We Are ✦

About HER Circle

A specialised organisation dedicated to healing, empowerment, and resilience for women who have experienced any form of crisis, conflict, or vulnerability — anywhere in the world.

Our Mission

Supporting Women Through Crisis — Towards Strength

HER Circle creates safe, culturally sensitive therapeutic spaces for women whose lives have been disrupted by disaster, armed conflict, forced displacement, domestic violence, or any other crisis situation. This intervention is adaptable across all cultural, linguistic, and geographic contexts.

The three central goals of every session are Healing, Empowerment, and Resilience. We believe every woman carries within her a reservoir of inner strength — and that creative expression and collective experience can help her access it.

  • Inclusive of all crisis contexts — disaster, conflict, displacement, domestic violence, and any vulnerability
  • Culturally sensitive and trauma-informed at every step
  • Grounded in six evidence-based therapeutic frameworks
  • Adaptable to any community, language, and cultural context
  • Committed to voluntary participation and participant autonomy at all times

✦ The Programme ✦

Activities & Session Flow

Each phase flows naturally — there is no fixed time. Facilitators allow participants to lead. The art activity continues until all drawings are complete.

Eight-Phase Arc

Session Journey

Phase 01
Welcome & Opening
TIC · Person-Centred
Greet participants warmly. Acknowledge their experiences. Establish a safe, confidential, non-judgmental space where every woman feels seen and valued.
Phase 02
Empowerment Song
Group Therapy · TIC
Stand in a circle. Sing together. Hold hands if comfortable. Sit on palm mats — non-hierarchical, familiar seating that honours cultural comfort.
Phase 03
Psychoeducation
TIC · Positive Psychology
Normalise trauma responses. Affirm strength and resilience. Connect emotional experience to the creative activity, using simple warm language.
Phase 04
Breathing Exercise
Somatic Grounding
3 deep breaths (4-count inhale, 2-count hold, 6-count exhale). Allow silence. Settle the nervous system. No equipment, no language barrier — universally accessible.
Phase 05
Hand Drawing
Expressive Arts Therapy
Each participant traces her hand on A4 paper and fills it with colours, shapes, and symbols representing her personal strengths and hopes. Soft music plays. No rush — all the time needed.
Phase 06
Colour Paper Strips
Positive Psychology · EAT
Each participant writes one personal strength or hope per coloured strip, to be pasted into the centre circle of the collective mural.
Phase 07
Collective Mural
Group Therapy · EAT
All hand drawings are arranged around the outside of a large circle. Strength strips fill the centre. Voluntary sharing — never pressured. A shared symbol of community resilience.
Phase 08
Reflective Closing
All Frameworks
Affirm the group. Guide 2–3 final deep breaths together. Thank all participants. Display the mural as a lasting symbol of shared strength and collective empowerment.

Breathing Guide

Simple Breathing Exercise

Allow silence after each round. Do not rush. No language, literacy, or equipment required — universally accessible to all participants.

🌱 Start — 3 Opening Breaths

1Sit comfortably. Hands in your lap.
2Gently close your eyes.
3Breathe in through your nose… (4 counts)
4Hold for a moment… (2 counts)
5Breathe out slowly through your mouth… (6 counts)
6Repeat 2–3 times until settled.

🌊 Middle — During Activity

✓Allow participants to breathe naturally at their own pace.
✓No instruction needed during the creative phase.
✓Invite them to stay present and notice how their body feels.

🌸 End — 3 Closing Breaths

7Guide 2–3 more deep breaths together.
8Breathe in… hold… breathe out slowly…
9Slowly open your eyes. You are here. You are safe.

Framework Details

1

Trauma-Informed Care (TIC)

SAMHSA, 2014 · Herman, J.L., 1992 · IASC, 2007
Core Principles
TIC recognises the widespread impact of trauma and integrates knowledge about it into all services. Built on six principles: Safety, Trustworthiness & Transparency, Peer Support, Collaboration & Mutuality, Empowerment & Choice, and Cultural, Historical & Gender Issues (SAMHSA, 2014).
Relevance to All Crisis Contexts
Whether participants are survivors of disaster, conflict, displacement, or domestic violence, trauma responses are physiologically similar: heightened stress arousal, hypervigilance, emotional numbing, shame, and disrupted self-worth. TIC is the container within which all other frameworks operate.
How It Works in This Session
Informed verbal consent is obtained before beginning. Confidentiality is established as a group agreement. Art sharing is always voluntary — never pressured. A referral pathway is prepared before each session. Palm mat seating creates a non-hierarchical space. Facilitators honour all cultural, religious, and community identities. Distress monitoring runs throughout.
Key Techniques
Verbal consent · Group confidentiality · Voluntary participation · Distress monitoring · Referral plan · Cultural inclusivity · Non-hierarchical seating · Warm non-clinical language · Never leaving a distressed participant alone
2

Expressive Arts Therapy (EAT)

Malchiodi, 2011 · Regev & Cohen-Yatziv, 2018 · Van der Kolk, 2014
Core Principles
EAT uses creative processes — visual art, movement, music, drama, writing — to support emotional processing and healing. Particularly effective where verbal language is a barrier due to literacy, language diversity, or the nature of traumatic experience (Malchiodi, 2011).
Relevance to All Crisis Contexts
For women affected by any form of crisis, trauma is often stored in the body — pre-verbal or beyond words. Art provides a symbolic language that transcends verbal disclosure and is culturally neutral. Any woman, from any background, in any language, can trace her hand and fill it with colour and meaning.
How It Works in This Session
The central activity — tracing her hand and filling it with colours, shapes, and symbols — is the primary EAT intervention. The hand symbolises agency, capability, and connection, all eroded by crisis. By reclaiming and decorating her own hand outline, each woman symbolically reclaims her body, her identity, and her capacity.
Key Techniques
Hand tracing · Colour, symbol, and pattern-making · Writing strengths on coloured paper strips · Assembling the collective mural · Soft music during activity · Voluntary sharing
3

Person-Centered / Humanistic Approach

Rogers, 1961 · Kirschenbaum & Jourdan, 2005
Core Principles
Every human being has an innate capacity for growth given the right conditions. The three core conditions: Empathy (understanding the client's inner world), Unconditional Positive Regard (acceptance without judgment), and Congruence (facilitator authenticity). The client is the expert on her own life (Rogers, 1961).
Relevance to All Crisis Contexts
Crisis — particularly domestic violence, displacement, and conflict — erodes a woman's sense of self-worth, agency, and trust. The humanistic approach directly counters this: unconditional regard communicates that she is valued exactly as she is. Non-directive facilitation restores autonomy.
How It Works in This Session
No participant is told what to draw, what to feel, or how to share. Participants are allowed to proceed at their own pace. The facilitator holds a supportive presence, not an expert authority. The closing affirmation celebrates every participant's individual strengths and the group's collective capacity.
Key Techniques
Warm empathic opening and closing · Non-directive facilitation · Participant-led pacing · Voluntary sharing · Sensitive reflective listening · Affirming individual and group strengths
4

Group Therapy & Counselling Dynamics

Yalom & Leszcz, 2005 · Burlingame et al., 2004
Core Principles
Group therapy harnesses the unique healing potential of group context. Yalom and Leszcz (2005) identified eleven therapeutic factors including universality ("others share my experience"), cohesion (belonging and safety), altruism (the healing power of giving support), installation of hope, and interpersonal learning.
Relevance to All Crisis Contexts
Isolation is a consequence of all crisis experiences. Disaster severs community ties. Domestic violence creates enforced secrecy. Conflict displaces women from their networks. The group format re-creates communal belonging. When women discover others share similar experiences, shame diminishes and hope is restored.
How It Works in This Session
The circle formation with optional hand-holding activates cohesion and universality. The collective mural — every woman's hand drawing placed around a shared circle — is the physical embodiment of solidarity. Coloured paper strips with personal strengths fill the communal centre, showing that collective strength is built from each individual's contribution.
Key Techniques
Circle formation · Empowerment song · Non-hierarchical palm mat seating · Collective mural assembly · Voluntary sharing · Affirmative group closing
5

Positive Psychology & Post-Traumatic Growth

Seligman et al., 2005 · Tedeschi & Calhoun, 2004
Core Principles
Positive psychology focuses on strengths, meaning, positive emotion, and hope rather than solely pathology (Seligman et al., 2005). Post-Traumatic Growth (PTG) theory recognises that survivors can experience positive psychological change — greater personal strength, new possibilities, and appreciation of life (Tedeschi & Calhoun, 2004).
Relevance to All Crisis Contexts
Crisis survivors are not only wounded — they are inherently resilient, resourceful, and capable of growth. Orienting toward strengths and PTG actively counters shame and victim-identity. It repositions each woman as a survivor with agency and a future — not a passive recipient of harm.
How It Works in This Session
The entire art activity is oriented toward strengths and hopes — not wounds. Each colour placed inside the traced hand is a deliberate act of positive self-identification. The psychoeducation phase explicitly affirms participants' existing strength and resilience. Every phase communicates: you are more than what happened to you.
Key Techniques
Strengths-focused psychoeducation · Hand drawing as strength-mapping · Writing personal strengths and hopes · Affirmative group closing · Collective mural as symbol of shared resilience and post-traumatic growth
6

Somatic Grounding: Simple Breathing Exercise

Levine, 2010 · Van der Kolk, 2014 · SAMHSA TIC
Core Principles
Trauma dysregulates the autonomic nervous system, triggering the sympathetic stress response. Slow, conscious diaphragmatic breathing activates the parasympathetic nervous system, lowers physiological arousal, and helps the body return to a regulated, present state (Levine, 2010; Van der Kolk, 2014).
Relevance to All Crisis Contexts
All trauma — regardless of cause — lives in the body. Before any psychological processing is possible, the body must feel safe enough to engage. The breathing exercise requires no equipment, no literacy, no shared language, and no cultural adaptation — universally accessible in every crisis context.
How It Works in This Session
Positioned between psychoeducation and the art activity as a physiological bridge. The 4-count inhale, 2-count hold, and 6-count slow exhale pattern activates the parasympathetic response. Three opening breaths and three closing breaths frame the session with intentional physiological safety.
Key Techniques
4-count inhale · 2-count hold · 6-count slow exhale · Repeat 2–3 times · Silence after each round · Natural breathing throughout art activity · No instruction during the creative phase
⚠ Section 8

If the Unexpected Happens

Stay calm. You are the anchor for the group.

Signs to Watch For
😢

A participant becomes tearful, shaking, or hyperventilating

😶

A participant becomes silent, blank, or dissociated

💬

A participant expresses hopelessness or distress verbally

🚪

A participant suddenly leaves the space

😰

A participant becomes agitated or overwhelmed

What To Do — Step by Step
1

PAUSE the activity quietly without drawing attention to the participant

2

Approach gently. Sit beside them. Speak softly.

3

Say: "You are safe. I am here. Take a slow breath."

4

If needed — step outside quietly with a second facilitator.

5

NEVER leave the participant alone at any time.

6

Continue the group session with remaining co-facilitators.

7

Document the incident fully after the session ends.

Ethical Commitments

Ethical & Cultural Principles

✓

Obtain informed verbal consent before beginning every session

✓

Establish confidentiality as a shared group agreement at the outset

✓

Sharing artwork is always voluntary — never pressure any participant

✓

Monitor for distress: tearfulness, shaking, dissociation, silence, agitation

✓

Hold a referral plan ready before every session begins

✓

Never leave a distressed participant alone at any time

✓

Close every session safely and intentionally

✓

Seek supervision if facilitators experience vicarious trauma

✓

Honour all cultural, religious, and community identities present

✓

Use warm, simple language — avoid clinical or formal terminology

✓

After every session: debrief, self-reflect, document incidents, celebrate what went well
